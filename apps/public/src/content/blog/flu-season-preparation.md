Every year, the flu sends millions of Americans to the doctor and hundreds of thousands to the hospital. But with a little preparation, you can significantly reduce your risk—and know exactly what to do if you or a family member gets sick.

## Get Your Flu Shot

The single most effective thing you can do is **get vaccinated every year**. Here's what you should know:

- **When**: September through October is ideal, but getting vaccinated later still helps.
- **Who**: Everyone 6 months and older should get a flu shot. It's especially important for adults 65+, pregnant women, young children, and people with chronic conditions.
- **How long it takes to work**: About 2 weeks for full protection to develop.
- **This year's shot**: Each year's vaccine is formulated to match the most common circulating strains.

## Build Your Defenses

Beyond the flu shot, everyday habits make a difference:

- **Wash your hands frequently** — With soap and water for at least 20 seconds. This is still the #1 way to prevent illness.
- **Avoid touching your face** — Flu viruses enter through your eyes, nose, and mouth.
- **Clean shared surfaces** — Doorknobs, phones, keyboards, and light switches.
- **Get enough sleep** — Your immune system needs 7–8 hours to function at its best.
- **Stay active** — Regular moderate exercise strengthens immune function.
- **Eat well** — Fruits, vegetables, and lean proteins provide the nutrients your body needs to fight infection.

## Flu vs. Cold: How to Tell the Difference

| Symptom | Cold | Flu |
|---------|------|-----|
| Onset | Gradual | Sudden |
| Fever | Rare | Common (100–104°F) |
| Body aches | Mild | Severe |
| Fatigue | Mild | Can last 2–3 weeks |
| Cough | Mild to moderate | Common, can be severe |
| Sneezing | Common | Less common |
| Headache | Uncommon | Common |

## When to See Your Doctor

Most healthy adults can recover from the flu at home with rest and fluids. But you should contact HCI Medical Group or seek care if you experience:

- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Persistent chest pain or pressure
- Dizziness or confusion
- Severe or persistent vomiting
- Flu symptoms that improve but then return with fever and worsening cough
- Worsening of chronic conditions

**For high-risk patients** (adults 65+, people with chronic conditions, pregnant women), call us at the first sign of flu symptoms. Antiviral medications like Tamiflu work best when started within 48 hours of symptom onset.

## Caring for Someone with the Flu

If a family member has the flu:

- Keep them in a separate room when possible
- Wear a mask when providing care
- Wash your hands after any contact
- Don't share cups, utensils, or towels
- Clean bathroom surfaces frequently

## Protect Yourself Year-Round

The flu is seasonal, but good health habits protect you all year. Regular checkups, staying current on vaccinations, and managing chronic conditions are all part of a strong defense.

High blood pressure—also called **hypertension**—affects nearly half of all adults in the United States. It's often called the "silent killer" because it rarely causes symptoms, even as it damages your heart, blood vessels, and kidneys over time.

The good news? High blood pressure is manageable, and understanding your numbers is the first step.

## Reading Your Blood Pressure

A blood pressure reading has two numbers:

- **Systolic (top number)** — The pressure when your heart beats
- **Diastolic (bottom number)** — The pressure when your heart rests between beats

Here's what the ranges mean:

| Category | Systolic | Diastolic |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Normal | Less than 120 | Less than 80 |
| Elevated | 120–129 | Less than 80 |
| High (Stage 1) | 130–139 | 80–89 |
| High (Stage 2) | 140 or higher | 90 or higher |

## Why It Matters

Uncontrolled high blood pressure increases your risk of:

- Heart attack and heart disease
- Stroke
- Kidney damage
- Vision problems
- Cognitive decline

Many of these complications develop slowly over years, which is why regular checkups at HCI Medical Group are so important—even when you feel perfectly fine.

## What You Can Do

Lifestyle changes can make a significant difference:

- **Reduce sodium** — Aim for less than 2,300 mg per day (about one teaspoon of salt). Read labels carefully—processed foods are the biggest source.
- **Exercise regularly** — 150 minutes per week of moderate activity (like brisk walking) can lower your blood pressure by 5–8 points.
- **Maintain a healthy weight** — Losing even 10 pounds can have a meaningful impact.
- **Limit alcohol** — No more than one drink per day for women, two for men.
- **Manage stress** — Chronic stress contributes to elevated blood pressure. Find what works for you, whether that's walking, reading, or spending time with family.

## Monitoring at Home

Your doctor may recommend home blood pressure monitoring. A few tips:

- Measure at the same time each day
- Sit quietly for 5 minutes before taking a reading
- Use an upper-arm cuff (wrist monitors are less accurate)
- Keep a log to share with your doctor at appointments
